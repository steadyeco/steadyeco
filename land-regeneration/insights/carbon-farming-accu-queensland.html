Carbon Farming — FNQ Guide

How Carbon Farming Works
in Queensland: A Landholder's
Guide to ACCUs

March 2026 8 min read Steady Eco Land Regeneration

What Is Carbon Farming?

Carbon farming is a way for landholders to earn income by changing how they manage their land — specifically by reducing greenhouse gas emissions or storing more carbon in vegetation and soil. In Australia, this is formalised through the Emissions Reduction Fund (ERF), administered by the Clean Energy Regulator (CER).

The unit of measurement is the Australian Carbon Credit Unit (ACCU). One ACCU represents one tonne of carbon dioxide equivalent (CO₂-e) either stored or avoided. Landholders earn ACCUs by running approved carbon projects on their land, then sell those ACCUs — either to the Australian Government at a fixed price through a Carbon Abatement Contract, or on the voluntary carbon market to companies offsetting their emissions.

For FNQ landholders, carbon farming represents a genuine and growing income stream — particularly for grazing properties with significant native vegetation potential and for landholders who have already invested in ecological restoration.

Note: Carbon markets fluctuate. The information in this guide reflects the ERF framework as of early 2026. Always seek independent financial and legal advice before registering a carbon project.

The Two Main Methodologies for FNQ Landholders

The ERF has multiple approved methodologies, but two are most relevant to FNQ landholders:

Methodology 01

Human-Induced Regeneration (HIR)

HIR generates ACCUs by removing the barriers that prevent native vegetation from regrowing — primarily by reducing or removing grazing pressure and stopping clearing. The land then regenerates naturally from its existing seed bank and root stock. This is a passive methodology: you change what you stop doing, rather than what you start doing. It works best on large areas (100ha+) with existing native species present. Very common on Queensland grazing properties.

Methodology 02

Environmental Planting

Environmental Planting generates ACCUs through active revegetation — planting native species on cleared or degraded land. It requires more labour and ongoing management but works on smaller areas and on land where the native seed bank has been lost. This is the methodology Steady Eco delivers. It suits landholders with cleared or heavily degraded areas who want to actively restore their land while generating income.

There are other ERF methodologies (avoided deforestation, rangeland reforestation, and others), but HIR and Environmental Planting are the two most commonly used by FNQ landholders seeking to combine ecological restoration with carbon income.

Is My Property Eligible?

Eligibility depends on several factors, and a full assessment requires a registered carbon service provider or aggregator — but here are the key questions to consider:

  • Has the land been cleared or grazed? Both HIR and Environmental Planting require evidence that the land was previously cleared or managed in a way that reduced native vegetation.
  • Is there native vegetation potential? For HIR, the land needs an existing native species seed bank. For Environmental Planting, you need access to locally-sourced native plant stock.
  • How large is the project area? HIR is generally only economical above 100ha due to the relatively low carbon yield per hectare. Environmental Planting can work on smaller areas — 10ha+ can be viable depending on ACCU prices.
  • Is there an existing carbon project registered? You cannot register a new project over land already covered by another ERF project.
  • What is the land tenure? Freehold, leasehold, and some Indigenous land tenures can be eligible. Certain crown land classifications may not be.

In FNQ, large areas of Wet Tropics fringe, Atherton Tablelands, Gulf savanna, and dry rainforest margins on Cape York are potentially eligible for one or both methodologies. If you have a grazing property with significant areas of remnant or regrowth native vegetation, HIR deserves serious consideration.

How Long Does a Carbon Project Run?

ERF carbon projects have a minimum permanence period of 25 years. This is a legal commitment: the carbon stored on your land must be maintained for the duration of the project. You cannot simply decide to clear the vegetation after five years — doing so would require repaying ACCUs to the government.

This is one of the most important things to understand before registering a project. Carbon farming is a long-term commitment, not a short-term income strategy. The landholders it suits best are those who intended to maintain or restore their native vegetation anyway — the carbon income is a bonus, not the primary driver.

Credits are not issued all at once. They are issued progressively as carbon is measured and verified by an independent auditor. For Environmental Planting projects, this typically happens every 5 years (or more frequently if you elect to pay for more regular audits). Steady Eco manages the on-ground monitoring, data collection, and reporting under the MERI framework (Monitoring, Evaluation, Reporting, and Improvement) for Environmental Planting projects we deliver.

What Is the Income Potential?

This is the question most landholders ask first — and the honest answer is: it depends heavily on methodology, project scale, land condition, and the ACCU price at the time of sale.

Illustrative Example — Environmental Planting

A 50ha Environmental Planting project in FNQ might sequester approximately 2–5 tonnes of CO₂-e per hectare per year once established (establishment takes 3–5 years). At a modest ACCU price of $30/tonne and a median sequestration rate of 3t/ha/yr: 50ha × 3t × $30 = $4,500 gross per year. At $50/tonne: $7,500/yr gross. Project setup, registration, and audit costs typically run $5,000–$20,000 over the life of the project. Net income depends on your specific site, methodology, and market price at time of sale. This is not financial advice — it is an illustration only.

HIR on a large grazing property can generate more volume at lower cost-per-tonne, but the per-hectare yield is typically lower than active planting. A 1,000ha HIR project is a different financial proposition to a 50ha Environmental Planting project.

ACCU prices are not fixed. They move with supply, demand, and government policy. The CER publishes forward contract prices for Carbon Abatement Contracts — which provide price certainty for a portion of your credits — but voluntary market prices fluctuate independently.

It pays for verified, measured, permanent carbon stored in the ground."

The Role of a Land Regeneration Contractor

A land regeneration contractor like Steady Eco is not the same as a carbon project aggregator or a registered carbon service provider. Here is how the roles divide:

  • Carbon project aggregator / registered carbon service provider: Handles ERF project registration, credit issuance, and sale of ACCUs. Often takes a percentage of credits in exchange for managing the administrative and legal process. Examples include Climate Friendly, Pinion Advisory, and others.
  • Land regeneration contractor (Steady Eco): Delivers the on-ground work — site assessment and preparation, species selection, planting, maintenance, and MERI monitoring and reporting. We are the people in the field making the carbon sequestration happen.

To run a successful Environmental Planting carbon project, you need both. The aggregator registers your project and manages the credit cycle. The contractor does the planting and keeps the vegetation alive and growing.

Some landholders choose to self-manage the administrative side (which requires more expertise and time). Others engage an aggregator who then sub-contracts the field work. Steady Eco can work in either model — directly with landholders who are self-managing, or as the field contractor under an aggregator arrangement.

Next Steps for FNQ Landholders

If you are considering carbon farming on your property, here is a sensible sequence:

  1. Talk to a registered carbon service provider first. The Clean Energy Regulator maintains a directory of registered service providers. Get an initial eligibility assessment — many offer this free or at low cost. This will tell you whether your property is eligible and which methodology suits it.
  2. Get a site assessment. For Environmental Planting specifically, a site visit is essential to understand soil types, existing vegetation, access, water availability, and species suitability. This is something Steady Eco can assist with.
  3. Understand the commitment. 25 years is a long time. Talk to your accountant and solicitor. If you plan to sell the property, understand how a registered carbon project transfers.
  4. Engage a contractor. Once your project is registered (or during the registration process), engage a land regeneration contractor to plan and deliver the planting program.
